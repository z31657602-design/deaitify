The tool wasn't finding AI content. It was finding writing that matched certain statistical fingerprints — fingerprints that countless human writers also have.

The Problem with AI Detection Tools

Let me explain what's actually happening here. AI text generators, including the ones powering ChatGPT, work by predicting the most statistically likely next word in a sequence. They optimize for coherence, grammatical correctness, and logical flow.

Human academic writers also tend to optimize for these same things — especially under time pressure, or when imitating the formal style they've been taught.

The result: AI detectors are essentially pattern-matchers. They flag writing that looks "too smooth," "too structured," or "too consistent." But those are exactly the qualities that good academic writing demands.

This creates a perverse incentive:

  • Students who write poorly get away with using AI (because their writing already looks erratic).
  • Students who write well — clearly, coherently, professionally — get flagged for doing exactly what their professors expect of them.

A tool built to catch dishonesty is instead punishing competence.

And it's not just my anecdote. A growing body of research supports this. Studies have shown that AI detection tools produce significant false positive rates, particularly for:

  • Non-native English speakers writing in formal academic style
  • Writers using structured templates or following disciplinary conventions
  • Anyone whose prose happens to statistically resemble common AI outputs

The consequences are real. Students have faced academic disciplinary hearings based on these scores. Researchers have had papers questioned. Lives have been disrupted — not because of cheating, but because a statistical model decided their words "looked wrong."

AI detection tools are probabilistic. They measure likelihood, not truth. A 90% score doesn't mean 90% AI content — it means the text shares characteristics with AI-generated text. That correlation isn't causation.
